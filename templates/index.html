Does the population density of a city affect it's transportation CO2 emissions?



The city of Springfield is a fast-growing middle-sized city in the US. Currently is at a turning point; this year has reached a population size of 300k and is experiencing a high rate of migration. The Urban Planning and Sustainable Development Office (UPSDO) is in charge of crafting a 10-year plan to guide the city's growth path between increased density or urban sprawl. Springfield is a city that cares about air pollution and its contribution to global carbon emissions and bases its decisions informed by data analysis. The city has requested its data analytics team to determine if increasing population density increases or reduces transportation carbon emissions.

Assumptions:
  • The variables population density and transportation CO2 emissions are highly correlated.
  • Higher-density cities will produce lower transportation emissions per capita assuming that urban design on those cities encourages alternative transportation methods such as walking, biking, public transit, and higher adoption the energy efficiency technologies (electric-powered transportation, and mass transit).



    • This map was created to give a first glance at the data for the top 98 (AK and HI were not included study) US cities.     How are a city's population and overall transportation emissions changing overtime?

    Five trends were observed when sampling over cities. First, the majority of cities’ population is growing. Second, in most cases, the population is growing at a similar rate to transportation CO2 emissions. Third, a handful of cities have managed to maintain a steady population growth while reducing their transportation CO2 emissions, examples are San Francisco, San Jose, Oakland, and Philadelphia. Fourth, another handful of cities have reduced their population and increased transportation CO2 emissions, examples are Detroit, Toledo, Memphis, Chicago. And last, there are cities that have reduced their population and transportation CO2 emissions, being Baton Rouge an example.

    Do larger cities have higher or lower transportation emissions?

    The scatter plot shows that cities with similar populations have similar transportation CO2 emission levels. And when comparing two outlier cities with similar population sizes, Houston and Los Angeles, we observed that Houston has higher emissions than Los Angeles. Maybe the differences can be attributed to emission regulations, being CA more stringent than TX. And when comparing Houston to NYC, a city with a higher population, we observed that Houston had higher emissions even with a smaller population.

    Does the population density of a city have an impact on it's transportation emissions?

    This bubble graph displays how each city is handling its transportation emissions output about its population growth. As we can see, cities with larger population numbers aren't necessarily the most significant offenders to emission output. Density seems to have a much larger play in this scenario. Most instances show that smaller population sized cities' results contain much more carbon emissions per person than cities almost ten times their population size. This graph shows that condensing population and a consolidated urban structure could be the most intelligent way for city planners to look to the future regarding transportation implementation for a greener life and fewer emissions output for all.

    Does the population density of a city have an impact on it's transportation emissions?

    • We were able to reject our null hypothesis, population density on a city has an impact on its transportation carbon emissions.
    • Some cities have managed to reduce their transportation CO2 emissions and continue it's population growth.
    • Due to more stringent state CO2 emissions and stronger urban growth boundaries, pushing for density.


    Recommendations for Springfield

    • Strong urban growth boundaries
    • Urban design for higher density
